About me

In 2020, after more than a decade as a nuclear astrophysicist and couple of years of reflection, I took the decision to shift to data science and move from Warsaw (Poland) to Dublin (Ireland). In Aug.-Sept. 2020 I took part in the S2DS (Science to Data Science bootcamp) where I worked together in a five-person team for SEGRO PLC (Europe’s largest investor in industrial Real Estate) to optimise their portfolio using:

  • Manipulation and Visualization of Geospatial Data (GeoPandas, Folium, Shapely),
  • Web-scraping (Beautiful Soup, Selenium),
  • Geocoding (Geopy),
  • extensive use of APIs.

I have worked for eleven years as nuclear astrophysicist with an internationally recognized expertise in the field of neutron stars. In practise I wrote codes in Fortran, C/C++ and Python (NumPy, Matplotlib, SciPy, …) and worked a lot of data visualisation (I have been very often praised for the quality and esthetics of my figures and the quality of my presentations and lectures) and used machine-learning techniques (linear regression, confidence intervals, correlations, calibration and optimisation of multi-parameter models) on a cluster.

Background

Neutron stars are born in the supernova explosion that marks the end of the life of stars with a mass around 10 to 30 times the one of the Sun. So far about 3000 NSs have been observed with telescopes and gravitational wave detectors. With a radius of about 12 kilometers and a mass of the order of 1 to 2 solar masses, they are one of the densest forms of matter in our Universe: the average density inside them is a couple of times larger than the average density inside heavy atomic nuclei. Their structure and dynamics are ruled by General Relativity. They owe their existence to the nuclear forces acting between the particles present in their interior and the properties of these forces at densities expected in the interior of neutron stars are mostly unknown.

My work was at the interface between theoretical and experimental nuclear physics and observational and theoretical astrophysics. Together with nuclear physicists I developed models for the interior of neutron stars, in agreement with nuclear experiments in laboratories, and simulate, taking General Relativity into account, various phenomena that astrophyscists could detect and observe with telescopes and gravitational wave detectors. This synergy between different fields of physics could ultimately allow us to understand the properties of matter inside neutron stars and of the nuclear forces in conditions not attainable on Earth.

I studied physics and astrophysics at École Normale Supérieure in Paris and at Paris-Meudon Observatory. In 2012 I got my PhD in astronomy and astrophysics with highest honors within the framework of a Polish-French cotutelle, meaning that I shared my time between Paris-Meudon Observatory (France) and Copernicus Astronomical Center in Warsaw (Poland). I then worked for a little bit more than a year at the Sapienza University of Rome (Italy) and then came back to Copernicus Center in Warsaw. From 2016 until the end of my career in Sept. 2020, I became a regular and frequent visiting scientist at Coimbra University (Portugal).

During my years as an astrophysicist I had the pleasure to travel the world for conferences and workshops and collaborate with scientists from France, Poland, Portugal, Italy, Russia, India, Brazil and more, resulting in 30+ articles in renowned international journals and 30+ presentations around the world, I conceived and organised 3 international conferences (50-250 participants each) and was a key contributor to winning grants (€1.2M in total). I also gave lectures in Physics at all levels across Europe and took part in many actions for the popularization of science (updates of popular books on astronomy, contribution to movies and animations about neutron stars, interviews by science journalists, translations from English to French of scientific articles and brochures). I also mentored women in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) from around the world.

More details on my research as an astrophysicist can be found on my academic website.
